How Matt Rife Earns And Spends His $30 Million Fortune

August 2024 · 8 minute read

Highlights

  • Matt Rife's net worth, estimated at approximately $30 million, comes from real estate, comedy tours, acting, and social media sponsorships.
  • Rife's "crowd work" in particular hastened his rise to fame, and Rife recently discussed crowd work with the Los Angeles Times.
  • Rife invests in stocks, real estate, and startup companies to diversify his income streams and grow his wealth.

Matt Rife, a young comedian and actor, has amassed considerable wealth in just a few short years.

He first shot to fame by participating in stand-up comedy shows, before being offered a role in the prominent sitcom Anger Management.

At the age of 28, Matt Rife's net worth is estimated at around $30 million, due to his success in comedy and acting.

Rife quickly made a fortune by investing wisely in real estate and raking in money through comedy tours, movie roles, and social media sponsorships.

Yet, with a promising future, he is still at an early career stage with significant room for more wealth accumulation.

Rife is one such youthful entertainer who had a meteoric rise to fame and became rich at an extremely early stage in life.

Updated, February 2024: On February 27th, the Los Angeles Times profiled Matt Rife and other young comics, all of whom shared their perspectives and insights on "crowd work."

What Is Crowd Work In Stand-Up Comedy?: According to The Gag : " ... also known as 'audience work' or 'working the room,' [crowd work] is when a comedian engages with the audience during their performance.

"This can involve calling out specific audience members, asking them questions, and improvising jokes based on their responses."

Matt Rife is a comic broadly associated with "crowd work," in part because he initially came to fame via brief clips he shared as "shorts" or "reels."

Speaking to the newspaper, Rife observed that the popularity of such clips online sometimes created the misperception that crowd work is "all [Rife] can do," and he explained how the content of his sets differed from his popular reels:

"They [audience members] think that because you only post crowd work that that’s all you can do, and it’s like, ‘No, I don’t want you to pay to come see me in person to see the exact same jokes you just saw for free.'"
